What is Ocean Acidification?


It is the phenomena in which the pH levels (acidity levels) of the ocean water begins to change as a result of the increase of the presence of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. When CO2 is absorbed by the ocean waters, it sets of a series of chemical reactions that results in the water becoming a lot more acidic and the decrease of carbonate ions present in the water. Note: The ocean is slight basic in acidity levels (8.2), however upon the rise of the Industrial Revolution from the 18th and 19th centuries, the oceans average pH now is 8.1. Since the late 1980s, about 95% of the ocean’s surface has now become more acidic, since the ocean absorb about 30% of the carbon dioxide produced and carried to the atmosphere.

Why is it a problem?
  • This phenomenon affects organisms like oysters, clams, sea urchins, deep sea corals and so on, as they rely on carbonate ions to maintain shells and structures.
  • Apart from that, this also negatively impacts important marine species such as planktons, as they are the basis of marine food chains. Without plankton, a lot of species would struggle to survive, and eventually the human species as well. Acidification also causes thermal stress in oceans, resulting in coral bleaching (it is when living corals turn white, weaken and eventually die), which will affect a lot of marine life directly.
  • It is not just an issue about marine biodiversity, it is also an issue of food security and economy.
What is the Causes:
  • One of the causes of acidification in the ocean is the use and extraction of fossil fuels, as well as the emissions from factories, boats and cars. Basically, anything that uses combustion to generate energy, which creates carbon that goes into the atmosphere and comes down to the ocean that exacerbates the problem.
What is pH and acidity?
  • pH is the measure of the acidity or basicity of a liquid solution. This is measured by determining the concentration of Hydrogen ions(H+) and Hydroxyl ions (OH-) on a scale from 0 to 14. Pure water has a pH of 7 and it is considered the bar for neutral, meaning it is neither basic nor acidic. A solution that has a pH lower than 7 is considered acidic, and a solution greater than 7 is considered basic.
  • The scale is logarithmic, so a decrease of one pH unit is a tenfold increase in acidity. (That’s why 8.2 to 8.1 is a concerning change.)
