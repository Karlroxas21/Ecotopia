Current Issues on Climate Change in the Philippines

Due to its geographical positioning, the Philippines is exceptionally susceptible to typhoons and other extreme weather phenomena. The impact of climate change has the potential to magnify the intensity of these occurrences, culminating in heightened frequencies of more severe typhoons, along with increased incidents of flooding and landslides.

The 7,100 islands that make up the Philippine Archipelago make it more vulnerable to typhoons and storm surges, which are abrupt elevations in water that affect nearby places during storms and typhoons.
The Asian Disaster Reduction Center (ADRC) estimates that the Philippines experiences 20 typhoons year, of which five cause significant damage.
Rapid population and economic growth are outpacing the water supply.

The substantial risk that rising sea levels, driven by climate change, pose to coastal communities in the Philippines is evident. This hazard has the potential to result in coastal erosion, the relocation of communities, and the depletion of valuable land and essential infrastructure.

Rising sea level threatens stability of boundaries, Philippine warns.
  • It is one of the region’s most susceptible to sea level rise brought on by human climate change because more than half of its cities and villages are located along its coasts.
  • Three times the normal worldwide rate, the Philippines has seen sea levels rise by 60 cm.
  • The World Meteorological Organization recently released statistics showing that the average sea level has risen more quickly since 1900 than it has in any other century in the previous 3,000 years.
  • Even if "miraculously" limiting global warming to 1.5 degrees, the globe will still experience a sizable rise in sea levels.

Climate risk threaten major industries & marine ecosystems in the Philippines coastal city.
  • The area's ecosystems are vulnerable, as seen by coastal erosion and a lack of natural aquatic safeguards. The city's coast is susceptible to erosion since it lacks coral reefs, coastal sand dunes, and seagrass beds. Mangroves, whose coverage has reduced owing to unlawful cutting, also support shorelines and marine environments.
  • Limited infrastructure for treating garbage from both industrial and household sources. Given the lack of a city-wide sewage treatment facility and insufficient solid waste management, risks to human health and the economy are posed by debris and water pollution from the fishing industry and informal river dumping of household waste through clogged canals, increased flooding, land subsidence, and contaminated bangus (also known as milkfish).

Ecosystems can face disturbance from climate change, putting the Philippines' diverse biodiversity in jeopardy. Vital ecosystems such as coral reefs and mangroves are in a state of vulnerability, impacting both marine life and the sustenance of coastal communities.

Mining and forestry – leading contributors to habitat destruction, as described above – is also an example of exploitative utilization of resources. Meanwhile, when it comes to wildlife, the demand for “exotic” plants and animals, for example, perpetuates the species decline crisis.
Waste and Pollution
  • Naturally waste materials and pollution also play a significant role in the decrease in biodiversity. According to the IUCN, pollution poses serious risks to the survival of bird (12%), amphibian (29%), and mammalian (4%) species. This includes pollution in all its forms, including solid, liquid, and gaseous.
  • Another significant problem that leads to the loss of biodiversity in the Philippines is chemical contamination. The most common form of these is organochlorines, which are utilized in farming and pest management.

The balance of ecosystems can be disturbed by climate change, posing a threat to the Philippines' abundant biodiversity. Coral reefs, mangroves, and other crucial ecosystems are in danger, influencing marine life and the means of living for coastal communities.

Climate Change will impact Philippines ability to feed its people.
  • Farmers and their families bear the greatest brunt of food insecurity and malnutrition in the country as prolonged droughts and extreme precipitation affect their crop yields.
  • In tropical nations like the Philippines, crop yields are predicted to fall by 25% as a result of climate change. Catch rates of the major fish species are also anticipated to drop by 40%.
  • More than 80,000 hectares of agriculture suffered crop damage during the eight-month drought, costing more than PhP2 billion (US$40 million) in damages. Farmers and their crops in the provinces of Quezon, Camarines Norte, Samar, Laguna, and Mindoro have also been impacted by the drought.

If the government does not include mitigation measures in future legislation, climate change is anticipated to decrease agricultural output and impair the supply of food.
Climate change is causing extreme weather events. Floods caused by torrential rain and powerful typhoons significantly destroy crops. Since they are happening more frequently, it is anticipated that they will worsen.
Climate change's consequences on agriculture include, among other things, pest damage, crop failure, and crop diseases. Insects damage more crops when temperatures rise because they consume food more quickly. As temperatures rise, crops also become more vulnerable to pests, which has an impact on their health. If these problems are not properly resolved, food production will decline, and it would be difficult to feed everyone.
Rice farmers in particular are suffering as a result of severe drought and flooding, to the point where planting is now viewed as an unsustainable means of subsistence because farmers cannot harvest and sell their products. In order to avoid working in agriculture, their children and the young people in rural communities who are meant to carry on farming and food production are now preferring to work in cities as wage workers, thus hurting the future and continuity of the agricultural sector.
The World Resources Institute predicts that by 2040, the Philippines would likely experience severe water shortages, with agriculture taking the most hit.

The effects of climate change can worsen health vulnerabilities, leading to the increased transmission of illnesses such as dengue fever and malaria, attributed to shifting temperature and precipitation patterns.

Climate Change impact health and Livelihood of Filipinos, Study says
  • The nation is regarded as one of those most susceptible to the effects of climate change. The dangers of vector-borne diseases like dengue and flood-borne diseases like leptospirosis are also rising as a result of increasing rainfall, stronger typhoons, and the resulting floods.
  • Drought-associated health hazards from reduced water availability and droughts, respiratory issues from air pollution, possible injuries from extreme weather events, and disease that spreads in shelters during catastrophes are all tied to climate change.
More public health crises
  • Increases in the prevalence of diseases including dengue, malaria, cholera, and typhoid are also brought on by rising temperatures. almost 40,000 dengue cases, 1,200 cholera cases, and almost 1,000 typhoid fever cases were reported nationally in 1998, the year the Philippines witnessed the biggest El Nino phenomena to date.

Unpredictable variations in rainfall patterns can result in both droughts and intense rainfall, impacting the accessibility of water for household, agricultural, and industrial purposes.
